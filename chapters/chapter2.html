Cosmology & Geology

2. Origin & Evolution of Earth

From the Singularity to the Blue Marble.

"We are all made of starstuff." — Carl Sagan

Key Takeaways

  • Universe originated ~13.8 billion years ago (Big Bang Theory)
  • Earth formed ~4.6 billion years ago from solar nebula accretion
  • Early atmosphere had no free oxygen — it was dominated by H₂, He, NH₃, CH₄
  • Moon formed from a Mars-sized impact (Giant Impact Hypothesis)

1. The Origin Theories

How did we get here? The answer has evolved from philosophy to physics.

A. Early Theories

1. Nebular Hypothesis (Kant & Laplace, 1796): Proposed that planets formed from a rotating cloud of gas (Nebula).
Criticism: Failed to explain why the Sun has 99.9% mass but only 2% angular momentum.

2. Tidal/Binary Theories (Jeans, Jeffreys, Russell): Proposed a wandering star approached the Sun, pulling out a cigar-shaped filament that broke into planets.
Criticism: Statistically improbable.

B. Modern Theory

The Big Bang Theory (1927): The Universe expanded from a Singularity 13.7 Billion years ago.

  • t=0: The Bang.
  • t=3 mins: First atoms (H, He) form.
  • t=300,000 yrs: Universe becomes transparent (CMBR).
  • t=4.6 Billion yrs: Solar System forms from a Nebula.

2. The Solar System Family

The planets are divided into two distinct groups based on density and composition.

Feature Terrestrial Planets (Inner) Jovian Planets (Outer)
Examples Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune
Composition Rocks and Metals (High Density) Gas (H, He) and Ice (Low Density)
Atmosphere Thin (Stripped by Solar Wind) Thick (Retained gases)
Goldilocks Zone Earth is perfectly placed. Too cold.

3. Star & Planet Formation

The 3 Stages of Planet Formation

Stage Process Outcome
Accretion Gravitational collapse of gas lumps in the nebula. Formation of a Core.
Planetesimals Condensation of gas around the core. Particles stick together. Small bodies called Planetesimals.
Coalescence Planetesimals collide and stick to form larger bodies. Protoplanets → Planets.

Modern Nebular Hypothesis (Otto Schmidt & Carl Weizacker, 1950)

Revised the original 1796 theory by Kant & Laplace. Key additions:

  • Sun was surrounded by a solar nebula with H2, He, and dust
  • Friction and collisions created a disk-shaped cloud
  • Planets formed through accretion (planetesimals sticking together)
  • This explains why planets orbit in the same plane and direction

5. Evolution of Earth's Layers

Lithosphere (Solid Earth)

Formed through Differentiation. While molten, heavy elements (Fe, Ni) sank to the core, lighter ones (Si, Al) floated to the crust.

Atmosphere (Air)

Stage 1: Loss of primordial atmosphere (H, He) due to solar wind.
Stage 2: Degassing. Hot interior released gases (H2O, CO2, N2, NH3) through volcanism.
Stage 3: Photosynthesis added Oxygen.

Hydrosphere (Water)

As Earth cooled, water vapor condensed. Impacts from icy comets also contributed. Rain filled the depressions for millions of years, creating oceans (~4 BYA).

Case Study: The Giant Impact Hypothesis (Theia)

The Mystery: Why is the Moon so large relative to Earth? Why is its composition similar to Earth's mantle but poor in iron?

The Solution: About 4.44 Billion years ago, a Mars-sized object named Theia collided with the proto-Earth ("Gaia").

The Result: The impact blasted a ring of debris into orbit. The heavy iron core of Theia sank into Earth (making Earth dense), while the lighter rock debris coalesced to form the Moon.
