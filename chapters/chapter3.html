3. Interior of the Earth

The inaccessible onion-layered engine of our planet.

"The center of the Earth is a place where the pressure is so great that the atoms themselves are crushed into a new state of matter." — Inferred from Seismological Data

Key Takeaways

  • Earth's interior is known through seismic waves (P-waves and S-waves)
  • Three layers: Crust (5-70 km), Mantle (2900 km), Core (3500 km radius)
  • The Mohorovičić Discontinuity separates crust from mantle
  • S-waves cannot pass through the outer core → it must be liquid

1. The Unknown: Sources of Information

Humanity has only scratched the surface. The radius of Earth is 6,371 km, yet our deepest drill is merely 12 km. How do we know what lies beneath?

1.1 Direct Sources

  • Deep Earth Mining: The Mponeng Gold Mine (South Africa) reaches ~4 km. Temperature makes deeper mining impossible.
  • Deep Ocean Drilling: Projects like the "Deep Sea Drilling Project" and "Integrated Ocean Drilling Program". The deepest drill is the Kola Superdeep Borehole (Russia) at 12 km.
  • Volcanic Eruptions: Magma brings material from the Asthenosphere (~400km deep) to the surface for analysis.

1.2 Indirect Sources

Most of our knowledge comes from inference:

  • Magnetic Field: Proves the existence of a liquid iron outer core (Dynamo Effect).
  • Meteors: Meteors are formed from the same solar nebula. Their solid iron/nickel cores suggest Earth has a similar structure.
  • Gravitation: Gravity (g) is not constant. It is greater at poles, less at equator. Anomalies indicate mass distribution.
  • Seismic Activity: The most important source. Analyzing wave paths reveals density changes.

2. The X-Ray: Earthquake Waves

2.1 Body Waves: P vs S

Feature P-Waves (Primary) S-Waves (Secondary)
Nature Longitudinal (Sound-like). Particles move parallel to wave direction. Transverse (Light-like). Particles move perpendicular to wave direction.
Velocity Fastest (~8 km/s in crust). Arrives first. Slower (~4.5 km/s in crust). Arrives second.
Medium Solid, Liquid, Gas. Solids ONLY. Cannot pass through liquid core.
Destruction Least destructive. Stretching/squeezing. More destructive. Creates troughs and crests.

2.2 The Shadow Zones

Seismographs located at specific angles from the epicenter do not record waves. This proves Earth has a liquid core.

  • P-Wave Shadow Zone (105° - 145°): P-waves are refracted (bent) by the liquid outer core, creating a "blind spot" band around the Earth.
  • S-Wave Shadow Zone (Beyond 105°): S-waves completely disappear beyond 105°. They strictly cannot pass through the liquid Outer Core. This zone covers 40% of the Earth's surface!

Case Study: The Kola Superdeep Borehole

Location: Kola Peninsula, Russia (Arctic Circle)

Project Duration: 1970 - 1989

The Goal: To drill as deep as possible into the Earth's crust (Target: 15km).

The Reality: Drilling stopped at 12,262 meters (12.2km). Why? Because the temperature at that depth was 180°C (356°F), far higher than the predicted 100°C. The rock behaved like "plastic," flowing back into the drill hole whenever the bit was withdrawn. This project proved that the geothermal gradient (rate of temperature increase) is not constant.

4. Volcanology: Intrusive Forms

When magma cools within the crust, it forms intrusive features:

Batholiths

Huge bodies of alternating magma, cooled deep underground. They form the core of large mountains (e.g., Sierra Nevada).

Laccoliths vs Lapoliths

Laccolith: Dome-shaped (mushroom) body. Magma pushes layers up.
Lapolith: Saucer-shaped body. Concave to the sky.

Sills vs Dykes

Sill: Horizontal sheet of intrusion (parallel to layers).
Dyke: Vertical wall-like intrusion (cuts across layers).
