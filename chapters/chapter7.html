1. Fluvial Landforms (Running Water)

Running water is the most dominant geomorphic agent on Earth. River landforms evolve through a predictable cycle (Youth, Maturity, Old Age), though real rivers are more complex.

1.1 Erosional Landforms

Landform Mechanism Characteristics
V-Shaped Valley Vertical downcutting by rivers in youthful stage. Steeper than glacial valleys. Characteristic of upper course. Interlocking spurs.
Gorge/Canyon Deep, narrow valley formed when downcutting is faster than valley widening. Steep, nearly vertical walls. Ex: Grand Canyon.
Waterfall River flows over resistant rock layer (cap rock) underlain by softer rock. Softer rock erodes, cap rock overhangs and collapses. Creates a Plunge Pool at the base. Recedes upstream over time.
Potholes Cylindrical holes drilled into the riverbed by swirling water and pebbles (Corrasion). Found on rock-cut river channels.

1.2 Depositional Landforms

Landform Mechanism Characteristics
Alluvial Fan Sudden decrease in river velocity as it exits mountains onto a plain. Cone/fan-shaped deposit. Coarse material near apex, fine material at edges.
Floodplain Repeated deposition of sediment during floods on the valley floor. Flat, fertile land adjacent to the river. Prone to flooding.
Levees Natural embankments along river channels formed by deposition of coarser sediment during floods. Raise the riverbed above the surrounding floodplain over time.
Meander River swings side-to-side. Erosion on outer bank (cut bank), deposition on inner bank (point bar). Eventually, neck is cut off to form an Oxbow Lake.
Delta Deposition of sediment where a river meets a standing body of water (sea, lake). Types: Arcuate (Nile), Bird's Foot (Mississippi), Estuarine (Narmada).

2. Glacial Landforms

Glaciers are rivers of ice. They shape high mountain and polar landscapes through erosion and deposition. They create U-shaped rather than V-shaped valleys.

Erosional Landforms

  • Cirque (Corrie/Cwm): Armchair-shaped depression where a glacier originates. Often contains a tarn (lake).
  • Arête: A sharp, narrow ridge between two cirques.
  • Horn: A sharp peak formed by 3+ cirques eroding into a mountain from different sides (e.g., Matterhorn).
  • U-Shaped Valley (Trough): A valley widened and deepened by a glacier. Steep sides, flat floor.
  • Hanging Valley: A tributary glacier's valley left "hanging" high above the main valley floor after the main glacier deepens its valley faster.
  • Fjord: A drowned glacial trough (U-shaped valley filled by the sea). Classic features of Norway.

Depositional Landforms

  • Moraine: Any glacial deposit. Types: Lateral (sides), Medial (middle), Terminal (snout), Ground (floor).
  • Drumlin: Elongated, teardrop-shaped hill of till. Steeper end faces the direction the glacier came from. Found in swarms ("basket of eggs" topography).
  • Esker: Sinuous ridge of sand and gravel deposited by a meltwater stream flowing *within* a glacier.
  • Outwash Plain (Sandur): Flat plain of sorted sediments deposited by braided meltwater streams beyond the terminal moraine.

3. Aeolian Landforms (Wind)

Wind is an effective geomorphic agent in arid and semi-arid regions where vegetation cover is sparse.

Erosional Landforms

  • Mushroom/Pedestal Rock: Wind-blown sand erodes the base of a rock more effectively (due to higher sand concentration close to the ground), leaving a mushroom-like shape.
  • Ventifacts: Rocks faceted and polished by wind-blown sand.
  • Yardang: Elongated ridges carved in soft rock, aligned parallel to the wind.
  • Deflation Hollows: Depressions formed by wind removing loose, fine sediment. Can create oases if they reach the water table.

Depositional Landforms (Dunes)

  • Barchan: Crescent-shaped dune with horns pointing *downwind*. Windward side is gentle, leeward (slip face) steep.
  • Seif (Longitudinal Dune): Long, linear dune aligned parallel to the wind. Formed by bi-directional winds.
  • Transverse Dune: Long ridges aligned perpendicular to the wind. Abundant sand supply.
  • Loess: Deposits of fine wind-blown silt, often very fertile. The Loess Plateau of China is the largest example.

4. Karst Landforms (Solution)

Karst landscapes are formed by the dissolution of solite rocks like limestone (CaCO₃) by slightly acidic rainwater (H₂CO₃).

  • Sinkholes (Dolines): Circular depressions formed by solution or collapse of underground caves.
  • Caves & Caverns: Underground chambers dissolved out of limestone. Home to stalactites (hanging from ceiling) and stalagmites (rising from floor).
  • Disappearing Streams: Streams that sink into the ground via swallow holes.
