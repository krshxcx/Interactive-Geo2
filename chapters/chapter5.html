1. The Deep Dive: What are Minerals?

A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an ordered crystalline structure. This is distinct from rocks, which are *aggregates* of one or more minerals.

1.1 Composition of Earth's Crust (Elemental)

Just 8 elements make up ~98% of the Earth's crust by weight:

Element Symbol % by Weight
Oxygen O 46.6%
Silicon Si 27.7%
Aluminium Al 8.1%
Iron Fe 5.0%
Calcium Ca 3.6%
Sodium Na 2.8%
Potassium K 2.6%
Magnesium Mg 2.1%
UPSC Critical: SIAL vs. SIMA

This elemental abundance explains the naming: Continental Crust (Si + Al = SIAL), Oceanic Crust (Si + Mg = SIMA). The crust is dominated by silicates. [Source: NCERT]

1.2 Major Mineral Groups

Feldspar (Most Abundant)

Makes up ~50% of the crust. Two types: Orthoclase (K-rich, pink, in granites) and Plagioclase (Ca/Na-rich, white/grey, in basalts). Used in ceramics.

Quartz (SiO₂)

Second most abundant. Very hard (7 on Mohs scale). Resistant to weathering. Used in glassmaking and as a semi-precious stone (Amethyst, Citrine). Related to Silica.

Pyroxene & Olivine

Mafic minerals (Mg + Fe rich). Dark colored. Olivine is the primary component of the upper mantle.

2. Classification of Rocks

Rocks are classified based on their *mode of formation* into three families:

Igneous Rocks (Fire-Born)

Formed from the cooling and solidification of magma (below surface) or lava (on surface). Called "Primary" or "Parent" rocks – all other rocks ultimately derive from them.

  • Intrusive (Plutonic): Cools slowly *inside* the crust -> Large crystals. Ex: Granite, Gabbro.
  • Extrusive (Volcanic): Cools quickly *on* the surface -> Small crystals or glassy. Ex: Basalt, Obsidian.

Sedimentary Rocks (Stratified)

Formed by compaction and cementation (Lithification) of sediments deposited by water, wind, or ice. Characterized by layers (strata) and often contain fossils.

  • Clastic: From broken rock fragments. Ex: Sandstone, Shale, Conglomerate.
  • Chemical: From precipitation. Ex: Limestone (CaCO₃), Rock Salt (Halite).
  • Organic: From organic matter. Ex: Coal, Chalk.

Metamorphic Rocks (Changed Form)

Formed from pre-existing rocks altered by **Pressure, Volume, and Temperature (PVT)** changes without melting. The process is called Metamorphism.

  • Foliated: Minerals aligned in layers. Ex: Slate (from Shale), Schist, Gneiss (from Granite).
  • Non-Foliated: No layered texture. Ex: Marble (from Limestone), Quartzite (from Sandstone).

3. The Rock Cycle

The Rock Cycle is the continuous process that transforms rocks from one type to another over geological time. It is not a one-way street; any rock can become any other type given the right conditions.

Cycle Pathways
  • Igneous → Sedimentary: Weathering & Erosion → Deposition → Lithification.
  • Sedimentary → Metamorphic: Burial → Heat & Pressure.
  • Metamorphic → Igneous: Melting → Crystallization.
  • Any Rock → Igneous: Melting (the cycle can shortcut).

4. Connect the Dots: Interdisciplinary Links

Chemistry: Silicate Structures

Most minerals are silicates based on the SiO₄ tetrahedron. These tetrahedra link in different ways (isolated, chains, sheets, frameworks) to form different mineral groups (Olivine, Pyroxene, Mica, Feldspar). Understanding this requires basic inorganic chemistry (bonding, coordination).

Economics: Strategic Minerals

The distribution of minerals like Lithium (batteries), Cobalt (magnets), and Rare Earth Elements (electronics) drives modern geopolitics. Understanding mineral formation processes (e.g., hydrothermal vents for copper/gold) is crucial for Resource Geography.
