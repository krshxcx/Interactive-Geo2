1. The Deep Dive: What is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity (Biological Diversity) is the variety of life. It exists at three interconnected levels.

Level Description Example
Genetic Diversity The variety of genes *within* a species. Provides the raw material for evolution and adaptation. Different varieties of rice (Basmati, Jasmine, etc.) or dog breeds (all *Canis familiaris*).
Species Diversity The variety of different *species* in a region. Most commonly measured aspect of biodiversity. The number of plant and animal species in the Western Ghats vs. the Thar Desert.
Ecosystem Diversity The variety of different *ecosystems* in a region (habitats, communities, ecological processes). India has deserts, rainforests, mangroves, coral reefs, alpine meadows – high ecosystem diversity.
UPSC Data: India's Biodiversity Status

India is one of 17 Megadiverse Countries, containing ~7-8% of all recorded species on only 2.4% of the world's land area. India has 4 of the world's 36 Biodiversity Hotspots: Himalayas, Western Ghats, Indo-Burma, Sundaland (Andamans). [Source: NCERT/NBSAP]

2. Threats to Biodiversity: "HIPPO"

The major drivers of biodiversity loss can be remembered with the acronym HIPPO, as popularized by E.O. Wilson.

Habitat Loss & Degradation

The single biggest threat. Deforestation, urbanization, agriculture, mining destroy natural habitats. When habitat is fragmented, populations become isolated and vulnerable.

Invasive Species

Invasive species outcompete native species for resources, often lacking natural predators. Ex: Water Hyacinth in Indian lakes, Lantana in forests.

Population Growth (& Pollution)

Growing human population increases demand for resources, driving habitat destruction. Pollution (air, water, plastic) degrades habitats and poisons wildlife.

Poaching & Overexploitation

Hunting, fishing, and harvesting species faster than they can reproduce. Ex: Poaching of tigers for bones and skin, overfishing of Bluefin Tuna.

Other (Climate Change)

Climate change alters habitats, shifts species ranges, and causes events like coral bleaching. It amplifies all other threats.

3. Conservation Strategies

In-Situ Conservation

Conservation of species *in their natural habitat*. The most effective long-term strategy as it preserves the entire ecosystem.

  • National Parks: Strictly protected areas for wildlife. No human activity/settlement (e.g., Corbett, Kaziranga).
  • Wildlife Sanctuaries: Protected areas, but some human activities (grazing, tourism) may be allowed.
  • Biosphere Reserves: Large areas with natural ecosystems (core zone), buffer zone, and transition zone for human settlement. Focus on sustainable development. (e.g., Nilgiri, Gulf of Mannar).

Ex-Situ Conservation

Conservation of species *outside their natural habitat*. A "safety net" for critically endangered species.

  • Zoos & Aquariums: Captive breeding programs (e.g., Panda, California Condor).
  • Seed Banks / Gene Banks: Store genetic material (seeds, sperm, eggs) for future use. (e.g., Svalbard Global Seed Vault).
  • Botanical Gardens: Preserve plant species. (e.g., Kew Gardens, UK).

4. Connect the Dots: Interdisciplinary Links

Law & Governance: Key Legislation & Treaties

Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD, 1992): The main international treaty. Sets goals for conservation, sustainable use, and fair sharing of genetic benefits. Led to the Nagoya Protocol. CITES: Regulates international trade in endangered species. India: Wildlife Protection Act (1972), Biological Diversity Act (2002).

Economics: Ecosystem Services

Biodiversity provides invaluable Ecosystem Services: pollination (crop production), water purification, climate regulation, medicines (e.g., Taxol from Pacific Yew tree). The economic value of these services runs into trillions of dollars, yet they are often treated as "free" and thus ignored in traditional economics.
