4. Distribution of Oceans & Continents

The restless dance of the lithospheric plates.

"The Earth is a mobile thing; continents are but rafts floating on a sea of magma." — Alfred Wegener

Key Takeaways

  • Wegener's Continental Drift (1912): Continents were once joined as Pangaea
  • Plate Tectonics explains earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain building
  • Three types of plate boundaries: Divergent, Convergent, Transform
  • Sea-floor spreading proved by magnetic stripe symmetry along mid-ocean ridges

1. Continental Drift Theory (CDT)

1.1 The Proposal

Alfred Wegener (1912) proposed that all continents were once formed a single supercontinent.

  • Pangaea: "All Earth" - The Supercontinent.
  • Panthalassa: "All Water" - The Mega Ocean.
  • ~200 MYA: Pangaea split into Laurasia (North) and Gondwanaland (South), separated by the Tethys Sea.

1.2 Evidence for Drift

Evidence Details
Jig-Saw Fit The coastlines of South America (Brazil) and Africa (Gulf of Guinea) fit perfectly. Bullard (1964) confirmed this utilizing computer mapping.
Rocks of Same Age Radio-metric dating shows rocks of 2,000 Million years in Brazil match those in Western Africa.
Tillite Glacial deposits from the Gondwana system are found in India, Africa, Falklands, Madagascar, Antarctica, and Australia.
Placer Deposits Gold mines exist in Ghana, but the source veins are in Brazil.
Fossil Distribution Mesosaurus (freshwater reptile) skeletons found ONLY in S. Africa and Brazil (across an ocean!). Glossopteris (fern) found across all Gondwana lands.
Critical Flaw: The force of drifting

Wegener failed to explain how they moved. He suggested:

  1. Pole-fleeing force (Rotation)
  2. Tidal force (Sun/Moon attraction)

Result: Rejected by physicists because these forces are too weak.

2. Post-Drift Theories

Convection Current Theory (Arthur Holmes, 1930s)

Radioactive elements in the mantle generate heat. Molten currents rise, hit the crust, spread out, and drag the plates. This provided the driving force missing from Wegener's theory.

Sea Floor Spreading (Harry Hess, 1961)

Observation: Ocean floor is not flat. It has Mid-Ocean Ridges (mountains) and Trenches.
Mechanism: Magma erupts at ridges, pushes old floor away, and consumes it at trenches.
Proof: Sediments at ridges are thin (young); sediments near trenches are thick (old).

3. The Data Vault: Plate Tectonics

The unifying theory (McKenzie, Parker, Morgan, 1967). The Lithosphere is broken into plates.

Plate Type Features
Major Plates (7) 1. Antarctica
2. North America
3. South America
4. Pacific (Largest, mostly oceanic)
5. India-Australia-New Zealand
6. Africa
7. Eurasia
Minor Plates Cocos: Between Central America & Pacific
Nazca: Between S. America & Pacific
Arabian: Saudi landmass
Philippine: Between Asiatic & Pacific

The 3 Plate Boundaries

  • Divergent: Moving apart. Creates new crust (Spreading Sites). E.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge.
  • Convergent: Moving together. Destroys crust (Subduction Zones). E.g., Himalayas (C-C), Andes (O-C).
  • Transform: Sliding past. No creation/destruction. E.g., San Andreas Fault.
Case Study: The Ring of Fire

Location: The rim of the Pacific Ocean.

Significance: Home to 75% of the world's volcanoes and 90% of earthquakes.

Mechanism: It is a continuous series of Oceanic-Continental convergence. The dense Pacific plate is subducting under lighter continental plates (like the Andes, Japan, Aleutians). This subduction melts the rock, generating pressurized magma that fueled the volcanoes.
