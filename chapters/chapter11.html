11. Water in the Atmosphere

From invisible vapor to torrential rain.

"Clouds are the visible evidence of the atmosphere's motion." — Luke Howard

Key Takeaways

  • Water exists in atmosphere as water vapor, clouds, and precipitation
  • Dew point: temperature at which air becomes saturated (100% RH)
  • Cloud classification: Cirrus (high), Stratus (layered), Cumulus (puffy)
  • Types of rainfall: Convectional, Orographic, Cyclonic/Frontal

1. The States of Water

Humidity Types

Absolute Humidity: Actual amount of water vapor (g/m³). Changes with volume.

Relative Humidity (RH): (Actual Vapor / Capacity) x 100. Percentage. Determines if rain will fall. Saturation = 100% RH.

Specific Humidity: Weight of vapor per weight of air (g/kg). Constant.

Dew Point: The temperature at which condensation begins.

Phase Changes

  • Evaporation: Liquid → Gas (Absorbs Latent Heat).
  • Condensation: Gas → Liquid (Releases Latent Heat). Powered by Hygroscopic Nuclei (Dust/Salt).
  • Sublimation: Solid → Gas.
Form Description Condition
Dew Droplets on cold surfaces (grass). Dew Point > Freezing Point.
Frost Ice crystals on cold surfaces. Dew Point < Freezing Point.
Fog/Mist Cloud near the ground. Mist contains more moisture (>75% RH). Inversion of temperature or warm air over cold surface.

2. The Data Vault: Cloud Classification

Luke Howard (1803) classified clouds based on Latin roots. Altitude determines the type.

Group Cloud Types Appearance & Weather
High Clouds
(6 - 12 km)		 Cirrus, Cirrostratus, Cirrocumulus Feathery, thin, ice crystals. White. Do NOT rain. Cirrostratus causes "Halo" around Sun/Moon.
Middle Clouds
(2 - 6 km)		 Altostratus, Altocumulus "Alto" prefix. Watery look. Altocumulus looks like "Sheep's wool" (Mackerel Sky).
Low Clouds
(0 - 2 km)		 Stratus, Stratocumulus, Nimbostratus Layered, uniform grey. Nimbostratus causes continuous drizzle/rain and darkness.
Vertical Clouds
(Grow Upwards)		 Cumulus, Cumulonimbus Cumulus = Cotton wool (Fair weather).
Cumulonimbus = Anvil shape, Thunderstorm, Heavy Rain, Hail.

3. Types of Rainfall

1. Convectional Rainfall

Region: Equatorial Regions.
Mechanism: Intense heating during the day causes air to rise rapidly. Cumulonimbus clouds form.
Nature: Heavy downpour, short duration, with thunder/lightning (4 PM Rain).

2. Orographic (Relief) Rainfall

Region: Windward side of mountains (Western Ghats).
Mechanism: Moist wind is forced up a mountain slope. It cools adiabatically and rains.
Shadow: The leeward side remains dry (Rain Shadow Area e.g., Pune/Vidarbha).

3. Frontal (Cyclonic) Rainfall

Region: Temperate Zones (Mid-latitudes).
Mechanism: Warm air rises over cold heavy air along a "Front". Common in Europe.

Case Study: Mawsynram (Wettest Place on Earth)

Location: Meghalaya, India (Khasi Hills).

Rainfall: ~11,872 mm per year.

Mechanism (Orographic): The funnel-shaped hills trap the moisture-laden Bay of Bengal branch of the Monsoon wind. The air is forced to rise steeply (adiabatic cooling), condense, and dump massive rain on the windward side. The leeward side (Shillong) gets much less rain.
