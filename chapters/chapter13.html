1. The Deep Dive: Ocean Basin Relief

The ocean floor is not flat. It has a complex topography shaped by plate tectonics, volcanism, and sedimentation.

Continental Shelf

The shallow, gently sloping extension of the landmass submerged under the ocean. Depth: 0-200m. Contains most of the world's fishing grounds, oil/gas reserves. Legal significance under UNCLOS (Exclusive Economic Zone).

Continental Slope

The steep drop-off at the edge of the shelf, leading to the deep ocean floor. Depth: 200-3000m. Average gradient ~4-5°. Cut by submarine canyons (often extensions of land river valleys).

Abyssal Plain

The vast, flat, featureless plains of the deep ocean floor. Depth: 3,000-6,000m. Covered by fine pelagic sediments (oozes and red clay). The flattest places on Earth.

Mid-Ocean Ridges & Trenches

Mid-Ocean Ridges: Underwater mountain ranges at divergent plate boundaries (e.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge). Site of sea-floor spreading. Oceanic Trenches: The deepest parts of the ocean (e.g., Mariana Trench ~11,034m). Located at convergent (subduction) zones.

2. Ocean Temperature and Salinity

2.1 Temperature

Average surface temperature ~17°C. Decreases from Equator to Poles. Decreases with depth (below the thermocline, ~300-1000m, it is uniformly cold ~4°C). The ocean stores and transfers vast amounts of heat, moderating climate.

2.2 Salinity

Salinity is the saltiness of water, measured in parts per thousand (‰). Average ocean salinity is ~35‰.

Factor Effect on Salinity
Evaporation Increases salinity (water leaves, salt stays).
Precipitation / River Influx Decreases salinity (freshwater dilutes salt).
Ice Formation Increases salinity (salt is excluded when seawater freezes).
Ice Melting Decreases salinity (adds freshwater).
UPSC Data: High & Low Salinity Areas

Highest (Open Ocean): Subtropical Atlantic (~37‰) due to high evaporation, low rainfall. Lowest: Near equator (high rainfall), in enclosed seas with river input (Baltic ~10‰, Bay of Bengal ~33‰), in polar regions (ice melt). Enclosed Highs: Dead Sea (~240‰), Red Sea (~40‰). [Source: NCERT]

3. Connect the Dots: Interdisciplinary Links

Chemistry: Salt Composition

The "salt" in ocean water is not just NaCl. It includes chloride (Cl⁻, 55%), sodium (Na⁺, 31%), sulfate (SO₄²⁻), magnesium (Mg²⁺), calcium (Ca²⁺), potassium (K⁺). This is related to the chemical weathering of continental rocks and hydrothermal vents.

International Law: UNCLOS Zones

The legal definition of the Continental Shelf is crucial for claiming Exclusive Economic Zones (EEZ). Disputes over islands (South China Sea, Senkaku/Diaoyu) are fundamentally about controlling the surrounding continental shelf and its resources.
