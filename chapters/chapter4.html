1. The Deep Dive: Continental Drift vs. Plate Tectonics

The question "Were the continents always where they are now?" has driven a century of geological debate. The answer is a resounding *No*.

1.1 Continental Drift Hypothesis

Proponent: Alfred Wegener (1912).

The Core Idea: Around 280 million years ago (Carboniferous Period), all continents were joined as a supercontinent called Pangaea, surrounded by a mega-ocean called Panthalassa. Around 200 MYA, Pangaea broke into two: Laurasia (North) and Gondwanaland (South).

Wegener's Evidence (Crucial for UPSC)

Evidence Type Description
Jig-Saw Fit The bulge of Brazil fits into the Gulf of Guinea on Africa's coast with remarkable accuracy (later confirmed by Sir Edward Bullard's computer analysis at 1000m depth).
Geological Similarity The Appalachian Mountains (N. America) are geologically identical to the Caledonian Mountains (Scotland/Scandinavia). They were once a single range.
Fossil Evidence Mesosaurus (a freshwater reptile) found *only* in South Africa and Brazil. It could not have swum across the Atlantic.
Placer Deposits Gold-bearing gravels on the coast of Ghana have no source rock in Africa. The source (igneous intrusion) is in Brazil.
Tillite Deposits Glacial sediments (tillites) of the same age found in South America, Africa, India, and Australia indicate they were all near the South Pole together.
UPSC Critical: The Fatal Flaw of Wegener's Theory

The Criticism: Wegener proposed that continents ploughed through denser oceanic crust like ships through ice, driven by tidal forces and Earth's rotation ("Pole-Fleeing Force" or *Fluchtpolkraft*). Physicists like Harold Jeffreys calculated that these forces were *far too weak* by many orders of magnitude. Without a valid mechanism, the theory was rejected until the 1960s. [Source: Dayal]

1.2 Plate Tectonics: The Modern Paradigm

The discovery of Sea-Floor Spreading by Harry Hess (1962) and the related paleomagnetic stripes (Vine-Matthews, 1963) provided the *missing mechanism*.

The Core Idea: The lithosphere (crust + upper rigid mantle) is broken into ~15 major and minor tectonic plates. These float on the weak, semi-molten asthenosphere and are driven by convection currents in the mantle.

Types of Plate Boundaries

Divergent Boundaries

Plates move *apart*. New crust is created as magma rises (Constructive Margin). Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge.

Convergent Boundaries

Plates move *towards each other*. Crust is destroyed (Destructive Margin). Three sub-types: Oceanic-Oceanic (Island Arc, e.g., Marianas), Oceanic-Continental (Subduction Zone, e.g., Andes), Continental-Continental (Collision, e.g., Himalayas).

Transform Boundaries

Plates slide *past* each other horizontally. Crust is neither created nor destroyed (Conservative Margin). Example: San Andreas Fault.

3. Connect the Dots: Interdisciplinary Links

Physics: Convection Currents

The driving force of plate tectonics is **convection**. The radioactive decay of elements (U, Th, K) in the mantle generates heat. Hot mantle material rises, spreads laterally, cools, and sinks – creating a "conveyor belt" that drags plates along. This is fundamental thermodynamics.

Biology: Biogeography as Proof

Why are marsupials found in Australia and South America, but placental mammals dominate the rest of the world? Continental drift explains their isolation. When Gondwanaland split, these landmasses separated, and their fauna evolved independently. Wallace's Line in Indonesia is a direct consequence of plate history.
