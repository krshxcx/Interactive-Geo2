14. Movements of Ocean Water

The restless dance: Waves, Tides, and Currents.

"The ocean is a river, circulating heat and life around the globe."

Key Takeaways

  • Three types of ocean movements: Waves, Tides, Currents
  • Tides are caused by gravitational pull of Moon (primary) and Sun
  • Spring tides = Sun + Moon aligned; Neap tides = perpendicular
  • Ocean currents redistribute heat: Gulf Stream warms Western Europe

1. The Vertical Movements

Waves

Waves are energy moving through water, not water moving itself (particles move in circular orbitals).

Driven By: Wind friction.

Key Terms: Crest (Top), Trough (Bottom), Fetch (Distance wind blows).

Tides (Gravitational)

Periodic rise and fall of sea level.

Driven By: Moon's gravity (strongest) + Sun's gravity.

  • Spring Tide: Sun-Moon-Earth aligned (Syzygy). Highest High Tides. (New/Full Moon).
  • Neap Tide: Sun-Earth-Moon at 90° (Quadrature). Lowest High Tides. (Quarter Moon).

📚 NCERT Data Vault

Waves: Wind-driven, water particles move in circles (not horizontal)

Tides: Moon & Sun gravitational pull + centrifugal force

Ocean Currents: Influenced by wind, Coriolis (gyres), density | Cold: Poles→Equator | Warm: Equator→Poles

2. The Data Vault: Ocean Currents

Horizontal movement of ocean water in a definite path. Like rivers in the sea. Driven by Planetary Winds, Salinity differences, and Coriolis.

Ocean Warm Current (From Equator) Cold Current (From Poles)
Atlantic (N) Gulf Stream (Strongest), North Atlantic drift. Labrador (Brings Icebergs to Titanic route), Canaries.
Atlantic (S) Brazil Current. Benguela (Causes Namib Desert).
Pacific (N) Kuroshio (Japan Current). Oyashio (Kuril), California.
Pacific (S) East Australian. Peru (Humboldt) - Key to El Niño.
Indian Agulhas, Mozambique. West Australian.
Currents & Fishing Grounds

Where warm and cold currents meet (e.g., Gulf Stream + Labrador near Newfoundland), oxygen is rich, plankton thrives, creating the world's best fishing grounds (Grand Banks). However, it also creates dense fog, dangerous for navigation.

Case Study: The Great Ocean Conveyor Belt

Thermohaline Circulation: A global system of deep-ocean currents driven by temperature (thermo) and salinity (haline) differences.

Route: Cold, salty water sinks in the North Atlantic -> travels south -> circles Antarctica -> rises in Pacific/Indian Oceans.

Significance: It distributes heat from Equator to Poles. It takes ~1000 years for a drop of water to complete the cycle. If this stops (due to melting ice caps fresh water), Europe could freeze (Day After Tomorrow scenario).
