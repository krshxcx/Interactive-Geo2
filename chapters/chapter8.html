8. Composition & Structure of Atmosphere

The blanket of gases that protects life.

"The atmosphere is a chaotic fluid system, governed by thermodynamics and gravity."

Key Takeaways

  • Atmosphere composition: N₂ (78%), O₂ (21%), Ar (0.93%), CO₂ (0.04%)
  • Five layers: Troposphere, Stratosphere, Mesosphere, Thermosphere, Exosphere
  • Temperature decreases at 6.5°C per km in the troposphere (Normal Lapse Rate)
  • The Ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbs harmful UV radiation

1. Composition of Air

Air is a mixture, not a compound.

Component Percentage (Vol) Significance
Nitrogen (N2) 78.08% Inert gas. Fixation by bacteria for plant growth.
Oxygen (O2) 20.95% Life support (Respiration). Essential for burning.
Argon (Ar) 0.93% Inert noble gas.
Carbon Dioxide (CO2) 0.036% (Rising) Greenhouse Gas (GHG). Transparent to incoming solar radiation but opaque to outgoing terrestrial radiation.
Water Vapor 0 - 4% Variable gas. Source of all precipitation. Decreases from Equator to Poles. Acts like a blanket.
Dust Particles Variable Act as Hygroscopic Nuclei for condensation (rain formation). Scatter light (Rayleigh Scattering -> Blue Sky).

📚 NCERT Data Vault: Key Facts

  • 99% of atmosphere's mass within 32 km of Earth's surface
  • Nitrogen: 79% (updated: 78.08% NCERT exact)
  • Oxygen: 21% (updated: 20.95% NCERT exact)
  • CO₂: Meteorologically important - transparent to solar radiation, opaque to terrestrial radiation (greenhouse effect)
  • Ozone Layer: 10-50 km altitude. Absorbs harmful UV rays (acts as filter)
  • Water Vapor: 0-4%. Decreases with altitude & from equator to poles. Absorbs insolation, acts as blanket
  • Dust Particles: Concentrated in lower layers. Act as hygroscopic nuclei for cloud formation

Atmospheric Layers (NCERT): Troposphere (weather) → Stratosphere (ozone) → Mesosphere → Thermosphere (Ionosphere) → Exosphere

2. The Layered Structure

The atmosphere is divided into 5 layers based on temperature trends.

1. Troposphere (0 - 18km)

Temp Trend: Decreases with height.
Lapse Rate: 6.5°C per km (Normal Lapse Rate).
Significance: Contains 75% of mass. All weather phenomena (clouds, rain, storms) occur here. Highest at Equator (18km) due to convection, lowest at Poles (8km).

2. Stratosphere (18 - 50km)

Temp Trend: Increases with height (due to Ozone).
Features: Ozone Layer (O3) absorbs UV rays. Free from clouds/turbulence → ideal for Jet Aircraft.

3. Mesosphere (50 - 80km)

Temp Trend: Decreases. Coldest layer (-100°C).
Features: Meteors burn up here upon entry due to friction.

4. Thermosphere / Ionosphere (80 - 400km)

Temp Trend: Rapid increase (up to 1500°C).
Features: Contains electrically charged ions. Reflects Radio Waves back to Earth (crucial for AM radio).
Auroras (Borealis/Australis) occur here.

5. Exosphere (400km+)

Temp Trend: High temp but very low density.
Features: Merges with space. H and He gases dominate.

Case Study: The Ozone Hole

The Incident: In the 1980s, scientists discovered a massive thinning of the Ozone Layer over Antarctica.

The Culprit: Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) used in refrigerants and aerosols. Chlorine atoms break down Ozone (O3) into Oxygen (O2).

The Solution: The Montreal Protocol (1987). A global treaty to phase out CFCs. It is the most successful environmental treaty in history. The Ozone layer is now slowly healing.
