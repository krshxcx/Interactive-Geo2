1. The Deep Dive: Humidity

Humidity is the measure of water vapor in the air. It is the source of all clouds and precipitation.

Absolute Humidity

The actual mass of water vapor present per unit volume of air (g/m³). This does not change with temperature unless water is added/removed.

Specific Humidity

The mass of water vapor per unit mass of air (g/kg). More accurate for meteorological calculations as it accounts for air density.

Relative Humidity (RH)

The **most commonly used measure**. It is the ratio of the *actual* water vapor content to the *maximum* capacity of the air at a given temperature (saturation), expressed as a percentage. RH = (Actual Vapor/Saturation Capacity) × 100.
Key: RH is inversely related to temperature. If temperature rises, capacity increases, so RH *falls* (without adding water). This is why deserts are hot but have low RH.

Dew Point

The temperature at which air becomes saturated (RH = 100%) and condensation begins. A high dew point means high moisture content. If air is cooled below its dew point, dew, fog, or clouds form.

2. Condensation and Cloud Formation

Condensation occurs when air is cooled to its dew point. It requires Hygroscopic Nuclei (condensation nuclei).

2.1 Cloud Classification (Luke Howard / WMO)

Altitude Cloud Types Characteristics
High (5-13 km) Cirrus (Ci), Cirrostratus (Cs), Cirrocumulus (Cc) Made of ice crystals. Thin, white, wispy. Cirrus indicates fair weather. Cirrostratus causes halos around sun/moon.
Middle (2-5 km) Altostratus (As), Altocumulus (Ac) Made of water droplets or ice. Greyish sheets (Altostratus) or puffy layers (Altocumulus). Often precede rain.
Low (0-2 km) Stratus (St), Stratocumulus (Sc), Nimbostratus (Ns) Low, grey, layered (Stratus). "Nimbo" = rain-bearing. Nimbostratus brings persistent rain.
Vertically Developed Cumulus (Cu), Cumulonimbus (Cb) Puffy, cauliflower-like (Cumulus = fair weather). Cumulonimbus = the "Storm King." Causes thunderstorms, lightning, hail, tornadoes.

3. Types of Precipitation

Precipitation occurs when cloud droplets grow large enough to fall under gravity.

Type Mechanism Characteristics / Location
Convectional Rainfall Intense heating of surface → Air rises rapidly → Adiabatic cooling → Condensation → Cumulonimbus clouds. Afternoon thunderstorms in tropics (Equatorial regions). Heavy, short bursts. Accompanied by lightning.
Orographic Rainfall Moist air forced to rise over a mountain barrier → Cools → Condenses → Rain on windward side. Heavy rain on windward slopes. Rain Shadow (dry) on leeward side (descending air warms). Ex: Cherrapunji on Western Ghats windward side; Deccan Plateau in its rain shadow.
Cyclonic (Frontal) Rainfall Warm, moist air meets cold, dry air at a front. Warm air is lighter, rises over cold air → Cools → Rain. Prolonged, steady rain. Associated with temperate/extra-tropical cyclones. Common in Europe, N. America.

4. Connect the Dots: Interdisciplinary Links

Physics: Adiabatic Lapse Rate

As air rises, it expands due to lower pressure and cools *without losing heat to surroundings* (adiabatic process). Dry Adiabatic Lapse Rate (DALR): ~10°C/km. Saturated (Wet) Adiabatic Lapse Rate (SALR): ~6°C/km (lower because condensation releases latent heat, slowing cooling). Understanding this is essential for predicting cloud formation altitude.

The Hydrological Cycle

Precipitation is one component of the global Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle). Evaporation from oceans → Condensation (clouds) → Precipitation (rain) → Runoff (rivers) → Oceans. It is powered by solar energy and gravity.
