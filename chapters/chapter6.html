1. The Deep Dive: Endogenic vs. Exogenic Forces

The surface of the Earth is a battleground between two opposing sets of forces. Understanding this fundamental dichotomy is the key to understanding landform evolution.

Parameter Endogenic (Internal) Forces Exogenic (External) Forces
Energy Source Earth's internal heat (Radioactive decay of U, Th, K). Sun's energy (drives climate, weather, water cycle).
Primary Effect Build up the land (Constructive). Create relief (mountains, plateaus). Wear down the land (Destructive). Reduce relief (erosion, levelling). This is called Denudation.
Processes Volcanism, Tectonism (Folding, Faulting), Earthquakes. Weathering, Erosion, Mass Wasting, Deposition.
Speed Often sudden (earthquakes) or slow (mountain building over millions of years). Generally slow and continuous.
UPSC Critical: Geomorphic Agents vs. Processes

A Geomorphic Agent is the *mobile medium* that does the work (e.g., rivers, glaciers, wind, waves). A Geomorphic Process is the *action performed* by that agent (e.g., erosion, transport, deposition). Do not confuse them. [Source: NCERT]

2. Weathering: The In-Situ Breakdown

Weathering is the breakdown of rock *in place* (in situ). It is the first step in denudation.

Physical (Mechanical) Weathering

Breaking rock into smaller pieces *without changing its chemical composition*.

  • Frost Wedging: Water enters cracks, freezes (expands 9%), and shatters rock. Dominant in periglacial regions.
  • Thermal Expansion: Daily heating/cooling causes expansion/contraction, leading to Exfoliation (onion-skin weathering). Common in hot deserts.
  • Salt Crystallization: Salt crystals grow in rock pores, exerting pressure. Common in coastal and arid areas.

Chemical Weathering

Breaking rock by *changing its chemical composition* through reaction with water, oxygen, or acids.

  • Oxidation: Iron in rocks combines with O₂ to form iron oxides (rust). Creates the red color in laterite soils.
  • Carbonation: CO₂ dissolves in rainwater to form weak carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which reacts with limestone (CaCO₃). This is responsible for Karst landscapes.
  • Hydrolysis: Water (H₂O) reacts with silicate minerals (like feldspar), breaking them down into clays.

Biological Weathering

Physical and chemical weathering caused by organisms.

  • Root Wedging: Plant roots grow into cracks and expand, physically breaking rock.
  • Burrowing Animals: Earthworms, termites, etc., expose fresh rock surfaces to weathering.
  • Lichens: Secrete acids that chemically attack rock surfaces.

3. Mass Wasting (Mass Movements)

Mass wasting is the downslope movement of material under gravity *alone* (no agent like water or wind as the primary mover).

Type Speed Material Characteristic
Creep Very Slow (mm/year) Soil, Regolith Imperceptible, cumulative. Bent fences, tilted poles.
Solifluction Slow Saturated Soil Found in periglacial areas where permafrost thaws seasonally.
Slump Moderate Soil/Rock Rotational movement along a curved failure surface. Creates a crescent-shaped scarp.
Landslide Very Rapid Rock/Debris Sudden, catastrophic sliding of material down steep slopes. Common in Himalayas during monsoons.
Mudflow/Debris Flow Rapid Saturated Mud/Rocks Fast-moving river of mud. Common in volcanic regions (called Lahars).

4. Connect the Dots: Interdisciplinary Links

Chemistry: Reaction Kinetics

Chemical weathering rates depend on temperature and moisture – principles from reaction kinetics. Van't Hoff's Rule (reaction rates double for every 10°C rise) explains why chemical weathering is fastest in hot, humid tropical regions.

Engineering: Slope Stability

Civil engineers must understand mass wasting. The angle of repose (the steepest angle at which loose material is stable) and factors like water saturation are critical for designing road cuts, dams, and buildings on hillsides. Ignoring these leads to disasters.
