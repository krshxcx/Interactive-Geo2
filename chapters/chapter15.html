15. Life on Earth

The intricate web of living energy.

"When we try to pick out anything by itself, we find it hitched to everything else in the Universe." — John Muir

Key Takeaways

  • The Biosphere = Lithosphere + Hydrosphere + Atmosphere (zone of life)
  • Ecology: study of relationships between organisms and their environment
  • Key terms: Habitat, Niche, Ecosystem, Biome
  • Food chains → Food webs → Ecological pyramids (number, biomass, energy)

1. The Ecological Hierarchy

1. Individual (Organism)

The basic functional unit. Survival requires adaptation.

2. Population

Group of individuals of the same species in one area (e.g., Tigers in Corbett).

3. Community

All populations (different species) interacting in an area. (Biotic only - e.g., Tigers + Deer + Grass).

4. Ecosystem

Community + Abiotic Environment (Soil, water, sun). The functional unit of nature.

5. Biome

Large regional ecosystem defined by climate and dominant vegetation (e.g., Tundra, Savanna, Rainforest).

6. Biosphere

The global sum of all ecosystems (Life Zone).

2. The Data Vault: Trophic Levels

Sunlight is the ultimate source. Energy flows unidirectionally (cannot be reused).

Level (Trophic) Role Example
Level 1 Producers (Autotrophs) Green Plants, Algae, Phytoplankton. Capture solar energy.
Level 2 Primary Consumers (Herbivores) Rabbit, Deer, Zooplankton, Grasshopper.
Level 3 Secondary Consumers (Carnivores) Snake, Wolf, Small Fish.
Level 4 Tertiary Consumers (Top Carnivores) Tiger, Eagle, Shark. No natural predators.
Decomposers Recyclers (Saprotrophs) Bacteria, Fungi. (Return nutrients to soil). Essential for cycles.
The 10% Law (Lindeman, 1942)
Only 10% of energy is transferred to the next trophic level. 90% is lost as heat (respiration/movement).
Consequence: Food chains are short (4-5 links max) because energy runs out. Top predators are few in number.
Food Chain vs Food Web

Chain: Linear sequence (Grass > Deer > Lion). Unstable.
Web: Network of interconnected chains. More stable (if one prey goes extinct, predator has alternatives).

3. Biogeochemical Cycles

Nutrients flow in cycles (Circular). Energy flows in a line (Linear).

1. Carbon Cycle

Source: Atmosphere (0.04% CO2), Oceans (Carbon Sink).
Process: Introduction via Photosynthesis -> Transfer via Food Chain -> Release via Respiration, Decomposition, and Combustion.

2. Nitrogen Cycle

Source: Atmosphere (78% N2 is inert/unusable).
Fixation: Bacteria (Rhizobium in legumes) convert N2 to Ammonia.
Nitrification: Ammonia -> Nitrites -> Nitrates (Plants absorb this).
Return: Denitrifying bacteria release N2 back to air.
